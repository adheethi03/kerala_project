TOP DESTINATIONS

KERALA BACKWATERS

The Kerala backwaters are a network of brackish lagoons and canals lying parallel to the Arabian Sea of the Malabar coast of Kerala state in south-western India. It also includes interconnected lakes, rivers, and inlets, a labyrinthine system formed by more than 900 km (560 mi) of waterways, and sometimes compared to bayous.

TEA PLANTATIONS

Munnar is a famous tea town in the Idukki district of Kerala. It is a hill station also which caters to a large number of tourists all the year round. The general elevation of the town is about 1600 m above sea level making it a very pleasant place in summers. The town is situated on the flat of the Kanan Devi Hills. Munnar is said to have received its name from the confluence of three rivers named Mudra puzha, Nallathanni and Kundala flowing very close to the town. The vast stretches of tea estates, rolling hills, beautiful valleys and the confluence of the three rivers make it a very popular destination for tourists in Kerala.

KERALA WATERFALLS

Kerala, often referred to as "God's Own Country," is home to several breathtaking waterfalls that draw nature lovers and tourists alike. Notable among them is Athirappilly Waterfalls, known as the "Niagara of India," located on the Chalakudy River in Thrissur district. Nearby, Vazhachal Waterfalls offers a serene environment perfect for picnics. In Wayanad, the three-tiered Meenmutty Waterfalls, one of the largest in Kerala, provides an adventurous trek, while Soochipara Waterfalls, also in Wayanad, is a favorite for rock climbing. Palaruvi Waterfalls in Kollam district, meaning "Stream of Milk," is renowned for its picturesque beauty and medicinal waters. These waterfalls not only showcase nature's grandeur but also serve as important ecological sanctuaries, offering a peaceful retreat into the natural world.

JADAYU EARTH CENTRE

Jatayu Earth Center, located in Chadayamangalam, Kerala, is a unique tourism destination that combines mythology, adventure, and eco-friendly tourism. The center is home to the world's largest bird sculpture, representing the mythical eagle Jatayu from the Indian epic Ramayana, which stretches over 200 feet in length, 150 feet in width, and 70 feet in height. The sculpture sits atop a hill and offers panoramic views of the surrounding landscape. Jatayu Earth Center also features an adventure park with activities such as rock climbing, zip-lining, and rappelling, as well as a cable car ride to the hilltop. The center is designed to promote sustainable tourism while preserving the natural beauty and cultural heritage of the area.

ABOUT KERALA

Kerala, located on the southwestern coast of India, is a state renowned for its stunning natural beauty, cultural richness, and progressive social achievements. Often dubbed "God's Own Country," Kerala is characterized by its lush landscapes, which include serene backwaters, picturesque beaches, and verdant hill stations like Munnar and Wayanad. The state's unique geography, with the Western Ghats on one side and the Arabian Sea on the other, contributes to its diverse ecosystems and abundant biodiversity. Kerala's society is marked by a high literacy rate, with nearly 100% literacy among its population, a remarkable achievement in the Indian context. The state also boasts an advanced healthcare system, with widespread access to medical facilities and a strong emphasis on public health, which has resulted in high life expectancy and low infant mortality rates. Kerala's Human Development Index is one of the highest in India, reflecting its commitment to education, healthcare, and social welfare. Culturally, Kerala is a vibrant tapestry of traditions and modernity. The state is famous for its classical dance forms such as Kathakali, known for its elaborate costumes and expressive gestures, and Mohiniyattam, a graceful dance form performed by women. Kerala's festivals, including Onam, Vishu, and Thrissur Pooram, are celebrated with great enthusiasm and showcase the state's rich heritage and communal harmony. The cuisine of Kerala is equally diverse, with a variety of dishes that emphasize the use of fresh coconut, spices, and seafood, reflecting the state's agricultural and coastal influences. Economically, Kerala is known for its spice plantations, particularly of black pepper, cardamom, and cinnamon, which have historically been significant in the global spice trade. The state's economy also benefits from remittances sent by a large diaspora, especially from the Middle East. Tourism is a major industry, with visitors drawn to Kerala's natural beauty, cultural experiences, and wellness tourism, including Ayurveda, an ancient system of medicine that originated in India. Moreover, Kerala has made significant strides in environmental conservation and sustainable development. The state's initiatives in promoting ecotourism, preserving its unique ecosystems, and implementing green policies have garnered international recognition. Kerala's progressive stance on social issues, gender equality, and community participation further sets it apart as a model for inclusive and sustainable development. In summary, Kerala is a state that seamlessly blends natural splendor, cultural depth, and social progress. Its achievements in education, healthcare, and sustainability, along with its rich cultural traditions and scenic landscapes, make it a unique and exemplary region in India.
